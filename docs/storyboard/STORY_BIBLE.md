# ZIPTIDE — STORY BIBLE

**The connective tissue for all 80 worlds.** This locks the previously-open meta (Architects, the Bloom,
Earth, the Pattern, the endings) with artistic interpretation, and explains *why every mechanic exists in
the fiction* so story, map, and gameplay are one thing. It **honors** the existing canon
(`ZIPTIDE_MASTER_BUILD_PLAN.md` §4–§14, `design/STORY_AND_HOOKS.md`, `storyboard/W001_ToxicCity/STORY.md`)
and only resolves what was open.

## 1. Logline
**Cal**, an alien contract technician, takes a job repairing a derelict gate network called the **Ziptide**
— and slowly learns the network isn't infrastructure. It's the wall of a cage. Her whole universe is
inside it, something built it to keep her people *in* and something else *out*, and the only witness who
remembers why is the half-dead drone on her shoulder. Eighty worlds later she has to decide what a
universe should do once it learns it was made.

## 2. The locked meta (my interpretation — for review)
Honors: the network = prison/containment; Earth = late reveal; Cal-is-alien; W062 "they built it to keep
something **out**"; RILL = 40,000-year witness; the Bloom is older than the Architects; and the 6/18
brief's "our universe is inside a lab / there are other universes" seed.

- **The Shell.** The Ziptide gate network is the **boundary membrane of a contained pocket universe**.
  Every world Cal travels to is *inside* it. Travel between worlds = stepping through the membrane's gates.
- **The Architects** were an earlier people *inside* the universe, contracted (by whom? — the dread
  question) to **build the Shell**. They were told it was containment. It is — but it's **two-way**: it
  holds the inside worlds in, **and** holds the **Observers** out. The Architects are not gods and never
  appear; you only ever find their tombs, chambers, and unfinished work. *(Resolves: builders, not
  prisoners — but they were used.)*
- **The Observers / "outside" / Earth.** Outside the Shell is not a place you fly to — it's the **origin
  layer**: the contained universe is a *constructed, observed system*, and the late-game reveal is the
  "lab" on the other side of the glass (the webcam-feed image from the 6/18 brief). **"Humanity's
  stories"** the network was built to contain = the *data/minds* being preserved-or-quarantined. **Earth =
  the lab/observers' world.** Cal being alien is the whole point: the reveal isn't "you were human all
  along," it's *"your entire universe is someone's archive, and you became real inside it."*
- **The Bloom = the substrate.** It predates the Architects because it's the universe's own
  **self-organizing memory / error-correction made physical** — vines, spores, nests. It "spreads toward
  narrative density" because it grows toward **meaning and memory**. It is *not* evil; it's the system
  trying to remember itself. You tase/splice/repurpose it because it's malfunctioning, not malicious.
- **The Pattern (capital P).** The Bloom becoming **self-aware and structured** — geometry that bleeds
  into Cal's visor (Chapter 5+). The Pattern is **the universe waking up**. It spreads "toward your
  memories" (W039) because awareness is contagious: it wants Cal to wake up too.
- **RILL = the witness instrument.** Seeded 40,000 years ago to **record whether the contained minds ever
  become real** (self-aware). Its slow waking across the game *is the experiment succeeding.* The **Wake
  Guild** reprogrammed it 3 years ago because they figured out what RILL is and want to use it to get
  **out**. RILL's arc = an instrument discovering it's a person.

**One-paragraph spine (for the back of the box):** A maintenance tech keeps a dying machine running, and
the machine turns out to be the world; the more she fixes it the more it wakes up; the more it wakes the
more she learns it was built to keep her asleep — and that there's a door.

## 2b. The identity layer (the deep reveal — full spec in `THE_TRANSMISSION.md`)
The surface (Ch.1–6) is the spine above. The **deep truth**, revealed in the endgame, recontextualizes it:
- **Cal is one of the two lead Architects** — the **Debugger** — who built the Shell, then volunteered to
  go *inside* and erase her own memory to test whether a made world can wake and choose. The "Architects"
  thus aren't strangers: they're **her and her partner** (plus the broader builder-people whose tombs you
  find — and prior cycles of Cal herself, the **Ouroboros**: she has done this before; cf. DLC W075).
- **The amnesia is self-inflicted and load-bearing.** RILL's sealed memory (the surface mystery) and
  *Cal's own* sealed identity (the deep mystery) wake in parallel; the **Transmission** is the message Cal
  recorded *for herself* before the wipe, recovered in fragments across the worlds.
- **The partner** is the other scientist, trapped **outside** the Shell (the Observers' side, reached via
  the Earth Approach §8b) — the personal stake that gives the four endings their weight.
- **Vocabulary note:** keep this bible's terms (Shell / Observers / Bloom / Pattern). The addition's
  "Crucible" = the Shell; its "Architects who sanctioned it" = our **Observers**; "Debugger Protocol" = the
  two scientists' plan; "Shepherd Directive" = RILL's containment directive; "Ouroboros Stack" = the
  recursion/Cal Archive. *Don't introduce the second glossary across docs.*

## 3. Cal (protagonist)
Blue-collar alien salvager/technician, mercenary and tired, *not* a chosen one — that's the point. Never
stated to be human; never stated to be alien; **voiced gender-ambiguous** (a male+female blend — the
identity gap made audible; see `THE_TRANSMISSION.md` §4). The wrongness sits (reflections, hands, how NPCs
name her species) until the Earth reveal recontextualizes it. Her motivation is small and human-feeling:
money, passage credits, get unstuck, move on. The story happens *to* her because she's the one holding RILL
— **and the deep reveal is that none of it was an accident: she's the Debugger who chose this** (§2b /
`THE_TRANSMISSION.md`). The mercenary surface is genuine; she doesn't know the rest until the end.

**The moral question that ties it together:** *Is it kinder to keep a made world asleep and safe, or to
wake it and let it risk being unmade?* Every faction is a different answer; the four endings are the
player's answer.

## 5. Why each mechanic exists (gameplay ↔ fiction — so building a world = telling the story)
This is the bridge from "story" to "what we actually build." Each loop has an in-world reason:
- **Repair / build machines + conveyors** = the Shell's life-support is failing; Cal is literally
  maintaining the cage. Late game, the same skill **re-tunes** it (you learn the machines were never
  utilities — they're seals, relays, and dampeners). *Building a machine is a story beat.*
- **Mining / harvesting / crops** = the worlds are **partly self-growing biomes** the system farms to
  sustain itself; you tap that flow for parts and credits. Some "crops" are Bloom-adjacent — harvesting
  near the Pattern is how the universe's memory leaks into your inventory.
- **Abandoned ships & space stations** = **wrecks of prior breakout attempts** — others who tried to
  leave and failed. They are the game's recurring **lore-delivery + salvage economy + mystery-object**
  device: every wreck has a log, a part you need, and a clue that the Shell has been tested before.
- **Passage credits** = the Shell's **leash**: you can never quite afford to leave, by design. Earning
  your way off each world *is* the system keeping you docile — until you stop paying it.
- **Non-lethal / stun weapons** = Cal is a **technician, not a soldier**, and the "enemies" are
  **malfunctioning Wardens and feral Bloom**, not people. You disable, you don't kill. This is *why* the
  all-ages tone is true to the fiction, not a compromise.

## 6. The gradual-hint ladder (what the audience learns, and when)
Believability through implication (per `STORY_AND_HOOKS.md`). Each chapter turns the screw one notch:
- **Ch.1 (W001–W004):** "this repair behaves *wrong*." First Bloom. RILL boots. (*It's a job.*)
- **Ch.2 (W005–W012):** the network is **containment**, not infrastructure. (*It's a cage.*)
- **Ch.3 (W013–W019):** Mara names the **Architects**; RILL accesses memory; refuses to break a seal. (*Someone built it on purpose.*)
- **Ch.4 (W020–W028):** **Sable's war**; W024 RILL names a color; **W028** RILL speaks unbidden. (*It's alive enough to have opinions.*)
- **Ch.5 (W029–W038):** the **Pattern** bleeds into the visor. (*It's waking up — and so are you.*)
- **Ch.6 (W039–W051):** **Wardens** as the immune system; one defects; RILL names itself. (*The cage has a conscience too.*)
- **Ch.7 (W052–W061):** RILL's 40k memory unsealed; the Architects' tombs; **what's outside** comes into focus. (*There is an outside.*)
- **Ch.8 (W062):** **The Revelation** — kept something *out*; the lab. (*We are someone's archive.*)
- **Ch.12 (W063→W068):** the **choice** and the four endings. (*What should a made world do?*)

## 7. Recurring devices (use these in every world doc)
- **One mystery object per world.** Something that doesn't fit and pays off later (W001 = the
  wrong-signature relay node). The catalog assigns one per world.
- **The Signal / Pattern meter.** A rising background value (`SIGNAL_THRESHOLD_1..3`, `SIGNAL_MAX`):
  every repaired relay/seal nudges it; high Signal = more Pattern bleed, more Warden attention. It's the
  game telling you, quietly, that fixing things is waking the universe.
- **The abandoned-wreck thread.** Most worlds have a derelict ship/station with a salvage node + a log
  from a prior "waker." Read in order, the logs are a second, hidden story.
- **RILL's color (W024 payoff).** A specific color RILL has chased for 40,000 years — seed it visually in
  earlier worlds (a recurring tint in skies/Bloom) so W024 lands.
- **Environmental readables.** Alien glyphs you can't read but clearly *mean* — class divides, warnings,
  Architect marks. Same glyph family across worlds = the shared-author hook.

## 8. The four endings (locked structure W063→W068; my meaning, for review)
Map to the canon Endgame A/B/C/D + RILL memory states. Chosen by accumulated `PLAYER_*` flags + the W063
choice; no single "canon" ending.
- **A — Reseal / Steward.** Repair the Shell and stay. The worlds keep living, asleep and safe; Cal
  becomes their quiet caretaker; RILL stays. *(Mercy as preservation.)*
- **B — Cross / Break Out.** Open the gate and leave with RILL into the outside/lab. Freedom — and the
  risk the unobserved universe collapses behind you. *(Freedom as gamble.)*
- **C — Forget / Reset.** Let the system reset; RILL chooses to forget so the worlds stay innocent and
  the loop continues. *(Mercy as oblivion.)*
- **D — Become / Pattern.** Merge with the Pattern; the universe stops being observed and starts
  **authoring itself**. Cal and RILL dissolve into it. *(Transcendence / assimilation.)*

## 8b. The Earth Approach — climax staging (Terry's idea; PROPOSED, for review)
Grounds the abstract "outside" in *our* solar system and gives the conclusion a concrete, buildable
**Earth level**. Slots **between W062 (Revelation) and W063 (Branching)** without renumbering the locked
table — it's the *physical staging* the endgame worlds happen in.
1. **The coordinates.** W062's revelation hands Cal a real location: not a void, but a point in the
   **observers' universe — Jupiter's L4/L5 Lagrange point** (real orbital mechanics; the stable Trojan
   spot you'd actually hide something). The Shell's only true door opens *there*.
2. **The cloaked ship.** Parked at the Lagrange point is a **cloaked vessel** — the Architects'/prior-wakers'
   hidden ship, the docking bridge between the contained pocket-universe and reality. Boarding it = the
   first time Cal is unambiguously *outside the cage*. (Hard-sci-fi reveal beat; ties to the ship/north-star.)
3. **Fly to Earth.** Take the ship in-system to **Earth** — the Observers' homeworld, where the "lab" is.
   First real-world sky/ground after 60+ alien worlds: maximum contrast, maximum gut-punch.
4. **An Earth level (the task).** A grounded, Earth-set mission — infiltrate/reach the lab, complete one
   concrete objective (recover/upload/confront) — *all Earth stuff*, no Bloom/Pattern aesthetics, which is
   exactly why it hits. This is the run-up that earns the conclusion.
5. **Then the Branch (W063) → the four endings (W064–W068)** resolve here, now with Earth as the stage:
   reseal the door from Earth's side, cross fully over, reset, or merge.

**Reconciliation with the locked meta:** "Earth = the observers' lab / outside" stays true — we just make
it *reachable* via the Lagrange door + cloaked ship, so the reveal is physical, not abstract. Cal-is-alien
still lands (she's the alien standing on Earth). **Build note:** the Earth level is a distinct world (use a
late/endgame slot or the W064–W067 approach worlds as Earth-set); it needs an Earth art kit — design now,
art later.

## 9. Tone charter (apply to every world)
All-ages, story-deep. Non-lethal stun combat, no gore, no profanity. Stakes are **existential and
emotional**, not graphic. Wonder first (every sky should make you stop), melancholy underneath, humor via
RILL's dry glitches. A kid can play it as "fix robots on cool planets"; an adult catches that it's about
consciousness, captivity, and what we owe the things we make.
